Language Experience of Monolinguals and Dual Language Learners During Preschool Classroom Activities

Van Pay, Choi, & Ryu, 2020
April 2020
PDF DOI

Abstract

Dual Language Learners (DLLs), who are learning two or more languages, are a rapidly growing population in the U.S., (Child Trends, 2014), and the rate of growth for DLLs in public education systems continues to exceed projections (Espinosa, 2013). These changes in demographics present opportunities and challenges in early care and education, specifically highlighting the need to ensure quality experiences for all children regardless of their language backgrounds. An initial study of the language experiences of young bilingual preschool-aged children of low socioeconomic status has shown significantly lower child-directed speech, conversational turn-taking with adults, and child vocalizations compared to their more affluent, monolingual peers (Wood, Diehm, & Callender, 2016). When examining specific activities in preschool settings that lead to high-quality interaction and language experience, multiple studies have shown that all activities are not equally beneficial for academic achievement. One study shows evidence for child-selected activities being most beneficial (Lippard, Choi, & Walter, 2018), another shows that large group instruction might be detrimental (Bratsch-Hines, Burchinal, Peisner-Feinberg, & Franco, 2019) and that meal-time can support decontextualized talk (Gest, Holland-Coviello, Welsh, Eicher-Catt, & Gill, 2006). Although there is some evidence for experience differences by language status and across activity types, there have been few investigations of the interaction of children’s language status and their language experiences during different activities. Using automatic language environment analysis (LENA) technology, this study aims to examine the language experience of preschool children across multiple classroom activities and examine possible differences in the classroom language experience between English monolingual children and DLLs. This leads to two quantitative research questions: 1. Do English monolinguals and DLLs differ in the amount of child-directed speech they hear from their teacher(s), is there a difference in the number of conversations they have with their teacher(s), and does the number of vocalizations they produce differ? 2. Does the language experience differ for English monolinguals and DLLs in different classroom activities (e.g., free play vs. small group)?
